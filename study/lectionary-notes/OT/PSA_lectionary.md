# Lectionary Notes: PSA

**37:1** — (no occasion text)

**2:1** — The Church includes Ps 2 in the Nativity (Christmas) Eve and Good Friday services. On Nativity Eve it is used in the refrain (called the prokeimenon or gradual) before the Epistle is read in the Divine Liturgy. On Good Friday, the entire psalm is part of the First Hour prayers.

**3:1** — Ps 3 is the first of the six psalms in the Orthros (or Matins) service. The other five are 37, 62, 87, 102, and 142.

**4:1** — (no occasion text)

**5:1** — (no occasion text)

**6:1** — (no occasion text)

**8:1** — Ps 8 is the communion hymn on Lazarus Saturday, the day before Palm Sunday, and it is also sung at Palm Sunday Orthros (Matins).

**11:1** — Ps 11 is a regular prokeimenon in the Sunday Liturgy, and it is also chanted before the Gospel reading in the marriage ceremony, as a prayer for the preservation of the union of husband and wife.

**12:1** — Ps 12 is an evening prayer, used in Great Compline and in the services of Great Lent.

**13:1** — Ps 13 is used in the Ninth Royal Hour of Great and Holy Friday, the hour of Christ's death on the cross. Portions of vv. 1 and 3 form the prokeimenon before the reading from Jeremiah.

**14:1** — Ps 14:1 is used in Orthros for the Feast of the Transfiguration.

**15:1** — Ps 15:3 is often used in the services of the Church to honor the saints.

**16:1** — Ps 16 is used in the Third Hour Prayers of the Church.

**17:1** — (no occasion text)

**18:1** — Verses 2, 3 are sung as the Alleluia before the Gospel reading in the Divine Liturgy on Nativity. And v. 6 is sung in the second canon of canticle five during Orthros for the Feast of the Nativity of the Mother of God.

**20:1** — (no occasion text)

**21:1** — Ps 21 is used throughout Great and Holy Friday, in Orthros, in the First Hour, and in Great Vespers. It is also used in the Feast of the Exaltation of the Holy Cross.

**22:1** — (no occasion text)

**23:1** — Verses 7–10 are proclaimed as the priest knocks on the door of the church on Pascha (Easter) morning, signaling our triumphant entrance into the kingdom of heaven through Christ's glorious Resurrection. This same action takes place at the consecration of a church building throughout the Orthodox Christian world. Also, verse 1 is said at the grave of the newly departed faithful, when earth is scattered in the form of a cross over the resting place.

**24:1** — Ps 24 is read in Great Compline and in the Third Hour Prayers.

**25:1** — (no occasion text)

**26:1** — Verse 1 is the prokeimenon before the Epistle reading in the Sacrament of Baptism, before the Isaiah readings at the Ninth Royal Hour and Great Vespers on the Eve of Theophany (Epiphany), and before the Epistle reading in the Great Blessing of the Waters. Verses 1–14 are also read as one of the psalms in the First Royal Hour on the Eve of Theophany.

**27:1** — Verse 9 is sung repeatedly in various services of the Church, and it is also woven into numerous hymns.

**28:1** — (no occasion text)

**29:1** — (no occasion text)

**30:1** — Ps 30 is used as one of the psalms in Great Compline.

**32:1** — Verse 1 is a communion refrain, sung on days of commemorating great saints of the Church. Also, verse 6 is sung on Pentecost Sunday in glorification of the Trinity. Then too, verse 22 is a favorite Orthodox prayer asking the Trinity for continued mercy. The abysses (v. 7) are deep, immeasurable places.

**33:1** — (no occasion text)

**34:1** — Ps 34 is prayed in the Third Royal Hour on Great and Holy Friday.

**35:1** — Verses 10, 11 are used in the Great Doxology, sung at the close of Sunday Orthros, and v. 10 is used repeatedly in the Forefeast of Theophany, identifying Christ as “the river of delight,” who was baptized in the Jordan. Also in the Creed, the Son is called “Light of light,” a phrase derived from this psalm.

**36:1** — Verses 30, 31 are used in the Church to commemorate St. John the Baptist and other followers of Christ who endured martyrdom.

**40:1** — Ps 40 is used liturgically on Great and Holy Thursday and Great and Holy Friday.

**41:1** — (no occasion text)

**43:1** — (no occasion text)

**44:1** — Verses 4, 5 are prayed by a priest as he vests for the Divine Liturgy, and v. 10 is recited in honor of the Mother of God as he prepares the holy gifts. Also, v. 18 is quoted in Lk 1:48 as part of the Magnificat, which is also sung in Orthros. Then too, this psalm is prayed in the First Royal Hour on Nativity Eve, and vv. 4, 5, and 8 are sung on the Sunday of the Samaritan Woman (see also Jn 4).

**45:1** — Ps 45 is prayed during the First Royal Hour on Nativity Eve.

**46:1** — Ps 46 is used in the services of the Feast of Ascension.

**48:1** — Ps 48 is used on feast days of the great fathers of the Church, for they exemplify what it means to be children of the kingdom.

**50:1** — Ps 50 is used daily in the services of Orthros, the Third Hour, and Compline, and it is also recited in every Divine Liturgy by the priest as he censes before the Great Entrance.

**53:1** — (no occasion text)

**54:1** — Ps 54 is prayed daily at the Sixth Hour and, in part, on Great and Holy Thursday and Friday.

**56:1** — (no occasion text)

**59:1** — Verse 6 is the inspiration for many Orthodox hymns concerning the cross of Christ.

**60:1** — Ps 60:6 is prayed as the evening prokeimenon of Sunday Vespers during Great Lent.

**62:1** — Ps 62 is the third of the Six Psalms (3; 37; 62; 87; 102; 142) in Orthros.

**63:1** — (no occasion text)

**64:1** — Verse 5 is used often in prayers for the departed and for their rest in the new city of peace. Also, verse 12 is incorporated into a hymn in the service of supplication on the first day of the church year, September 1.

**65:1** — Ps 65 forms a unit with 66 and 67, which are used throughout the services of Pascha and Bright Week.

**66:1** — Ps 66 forms a unit with 65 and 67, which are used throughout the services of Pascha and Bright Week.

**67:1** — Ps 67 forms a unit with 65 and 66, which are used throughout the services of Pascha and Bright Week. And vv. 2–4 are sung all through the paschal season with the refrain, Christ is risen from the dead, trampling down death by death, and upon those in the tombs bestowing life.

**68:1** — Verses 18, 19 are used in the service of Forgiveness Sunday to usher in the season of Great Lent.

**69:1** — Ps 69 is read in both Little Compline and Great Compline.

**71:1** — Ps 71 is used all through the Nativity services.

**73:1** — (no occasion text)

**76:1** — Verses 14b, 15a are sung as a refrain in Vespers at Pascha, Pentecost, and Nativity.

**79:1** — (no occasion text)

**81:1** — Ps 81 is sung on Holy Saturday at the Liturgy of St. Basil, and again at Great Vespers, with v. 8 repeated as a refrain after each verse. Verse 6 is also quoted by Christ in Jn 10:34.

**83:1** — Ps 83 is read in the Ninth Hour each day, and also at the Feast of the Transfiguration.

**84:1** — Ps 84 is read at the Ninth Hour each day.

**85:1** — Ps 85 is read each day at the Ninth Hour.

**86:1** — Verse 5 is used as the prokeimenon before the Isaiah reading at the Ninth Royal Hour on Nativity Eve. Also, the verses of the entire psalm are used as refrains before the Isaiah 11 reading in Great Vespers.

**87:1** — Ps 87 is prayed in the Service of Lamentations in Holy Week. It is also one of the six psalms of Orthros. The other five are 3; 37; 62; 102; 142.

**88:1** — Verse 13 is sung both in Great Vespers and in the Divine Liturgy of the Feast of the Transfiguration.

**89:1** — Ps 89 is read daily at the First Hour.

**90:1** — Ps 90 is read at the Sixth Hour each day, along with 53 and 54. It is also read in Great Compline and in the funeral service.

**91:1** — Verses 2, 3, 5 are sung as the First Antiphon (psalm sung responsively) on a regular Sunday.

**92:1** — At every Great Vespers service (including the Feast of Theophany), v. 1 is sung. Ps 92 is also read in the Ninth Royal Hour of the Feast of Theophany.

**94:1** — Ps 94 is sung responsively in the Third Antiphon of the Divine Liturgy.

**97:1** — Verses 2 and 3 are sung at the Feast of Christ's Presentation in the Temple, and other portions of the psalm are sung on Palm Sunday. Both feasts emphasize the two comings of Christ.

**98:1** — (no occasion text)

**100:1** — This psalm is read daily in the morning at the First Hour, along with Ps 5 and 89.

**101:1** — Verse 14 is quoted in the Divine Liturgy on Pascha morning before the reading of the Holy Gospel.

**102:1** — This psalm is the fifth of the six Matins psalms (3; 37; 62; 87; 102; 142). It is often used in the First Antiphon of the Divine Liturgy. Verses 20, 21 are also used on days when angels are honored in the Church.

**103:1** — This psalm is the great psalm of Vespers. Because of v. 30, this psalm is also used on the Day of Pentecost.

**107:1** — (no occasion text)

**108:1** — Ps 108 is prayed at the Third Royal Hour on Great and Holy Friday.

**109:1** — Verse 3 is used throughout the services of the Nativity season.

**110:1** — Verse 9 is used as a Communion hymn in the Divine Liturgy on Nativity.

**111:1** — (no occasion text)

**112:1** — Verse 2 is sung at the close of the Divine Liturgy.

**113:1** — This psalm is used in the celebration of the Feast of Theophany, and at other feasts as well.

**114:1** — This psalm is used on Palm Sunday with reference to the resurrection of Lazarus the day before. His resurrection forecasts the resurrection of us all.

**115:1** — This psalm is one of several Communion hymns in the Eucharist. Verse 5 is sung at the feasts of the Mother of God and of the saints.

**116:1** — This psalm is used liturgically each day at Vespers.

**117:1** — This psalm is rich in the liturgical usage of the Church. For example, v. 24 is sung on Pascha; vv. 25, 26a are sung on Palm Sunday; and vv. 26, 27a are sung daily at Orthros.

**118:1** — This psalm is prayed in its entirety at the Lamentations of Orthros on Great and Holy Saturday, when the Church assembles around the tomb of Christ. Further, this psalm is the core of the Church's funeral service.

**119:1-1** — In addition, Ps 129 is a daily vesperal hymn. Ps 131, a messianic psalm, is used at Nativity and for the feasts of the Mother of God. Verse 11 of this psalm shows that God the Son became Man in her womb.

**134:1-1** — These two psalms form the core of Orthros for the great feast days of the Church.

**136:1** — In this context of exile, it is used in the Orthros service beginning just before Great Lent on the Sunday of the Prodigal Son (the exile returning home), and throughout the Lenten Season in the Matins services.

**137:1** — This psalm is used on feast days honoring the angels.

**139:1** — (no occasion text)

**142:1** — This psalm is the last of the Six Psalms of Orthros. The other five are 3; 37; 62; 87; 102. Verse 10b is used in the Church at the Feast of Pentecost.

**144:1** — (no occasion text)

**145:1** — (no occasion text)

**148:1-1** — These psalms are sung every morning in the Church as the Praises of Orthros.
